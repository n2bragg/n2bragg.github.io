Page Numbers are meant to help, not cause confusion

Location: Home > UI / UX Commentary > Page Numbers are meant to help, not cause confusion (Fuji Example)

Here we see an example with name confusion. This is the same problem when a single street has two or more names, and multiple streets in the same city have identical names. This page numbering scheme will only cause confusion.

Let's take a moment to imagine a phone call between a customer and technical support:


Tech: Please open the manual to page number one.
Customer: OK, I'm there.
Tech: Here you will see a list of the supplied accessories, the first two items are the battery and battery charger.
Customer: Wait, I don't see the accessories.
Tech: Turn to page one please.
Customer: I am on page one.
Tech: Here you will see a list of the supplied accessories, the first two items are the battery and battery charger.
Customer: I don't see the accessories! I see a picture of the camera.
Tech: Are you looking at the manual?
Customer: Yes, I found a .pdf file on google.
Tech: Are you looking at the page numbers in the lower right hand corner?
Customer: No, mine are at the top.
Tech: OK, look in the lower right hand corner please.
Customer: There aren't any page numbers.
Tech: Scroll down.
Customer: OK, now I see we are on page. 17
Tech: OK, great, you see the accessories listed...

This is obviously frustrating for the user and costly to the manufacturer.

The solution is very simple. Recognize that users may interact with your product multiple ways, and put yourself in their shoes. In this case giving each page a single undisputed page number makes life easier for everyone.
